Similar Pairs of Gloves, Distinct Wearers

Identifying GK profiles using data based on their play styles
and importance to their team for the 2021/22 Premier League
Season

Introduction

Goalkeepers prevent the opponents from scoring. They are the last line of defence, the ‘Guardian Angel’ of a team. As long as they can keep the ball out of their own net, the team cannot lose a match. However, this feels like an inadequate description. The role of goalkeeper has evolved the most in the last decade. The responsibility of a modern-day keeper lies beyond just stopping the opposition’s goal attempts. Goalkeepers are no longer an isolated team member, away from all of the action. They are involved in every passage of play: building up the attack, commanding a high defensive line, beating the opposition press, providing an outlet for progression both short and long to name a few. A shift in football ideologies has propelled the importance of goalkeepers to a higher level. The purpose of this article is to sort Premier League keepers into separate categories, which can hopefully be used for scouting similar profiles of players. This will help us identify the requirement and style of the team they play for and their strengths and weaknesses. We can form an opinion of what to expect and what not to expect from a certain profile of goalkeeper.

Metrics for analysis

Traditional metrics such as saves and clean sheets are not enough to value and quantify the contribution of a goalkeeper. Unfortunately, not much detailed data is available directly to assess goalkeepers. But with some searching, filtering and formulating we have availed data that can value goalkeeper contributions much better than traditional stats. However, by no means this data is an exact measurement of a goalkeeper's skill. We can only arrive at a general idea of that keeper's skillset. The metrics have been divided into four broad areas:

  • Shot-Stopping
  • Box Control
  • Sweeping
  • Ball-Playing

Shot-Stopping

Post-Shot xG values the quality of the shot faced by the keeper, after the ball has left the foot (or body part) of the attacking player. High PSxG value signifies that the keeper has faced high quality shots. For this part we will consider the PSxG +/- which is calculated by (PSxG minus xG).
Goals Saved Above Average % measures the ability of the keeper to save shots. The model first computes the number of goals an average goalkeeper would concede for that given xG. Then it helps us to figure out whether our keeper has saved higher or lower than the average value.


Box Control

Crosses Stopped % is the measure of stopped ball entries into the box from wide areas.
High Claims p90 is the number of high ball collects performed by the keeper from balls over the defence, set-pieces and crosses.


Sweeping

Defensive Actions p90 performed outside the penalty area such as claiming, punching the ball, getting forward to reduce the shot angle or challenge a 1v1.
Avg. Distance of Actions of both inside and outside the penalty area. This gives an idea if the keeper strictly sticks to his line or if he does not hesitate to perform defensive actions to help out their defence.


Ball-Playing

Build Up Involvement frequency of when goalkeeper has successfully played the ball to his teammates in live ball situations (i.e., excluding goal kicks, freekicks, corners and throw-ins)
Long Range Distribution is the completion rate of successful long passes over 40 yards distance towards the opponent half (this is a flawed metric because I could not separate long passes in dead ball situations from this stat).


Types of Goalkeepers in the Premier League

Line Goalkeepers:

Line Goalkeepers are very athletic, excellent shot-stoppers and are equipped with lightning reflexes to recover quickly after blocking a shot. They are usually seen making acrobatic saves and multiple blocks in a short span of time. As the name suggests, these category of GKs are dedicated to staying deep near their goal line at all times. Line keepers have very limited technical ability on the ball. They are not involved in any build-up play only possess simple skill suitable for long goal kicks and freekicks. They also do not help out their defensive lines by coming out for claims and interceptions.
Example: David de Gea



Classic Goalkeepers:

Classic goalkeepers are good at controlling their box by stopping crosses. They are able to circulate possession to beat the opposition press, but usually not much involved in the build-up phase. Their ball playing ability is above average and are able to reach both short and long range zones if needed. Classic GKs are also very good at shot-stopping
Example: Edouard Mendy, Martin Dúbravka



Anticipative Goalkeepers

Similar to classic keepers, Anticipative GKs are very dominant inside the penalty area, but also outside their box. They are aggressive in their positioning, able to manoeuvre easily and also have an elite ability to read the play and ball trajectories. Therefore, they can anticipate and stop opposition attacks with expert accuracy. Anticipative keepers are effective in playing for teams that are dominated in possession and concede a lot of chances. They have a big presence between the sticks but average passing technique. But they can swiftly launch counter attacks against the run of play to help out their team.
Example: Nick Pope, José Sá



Proactive Goalkeepers

Proactive Goalkeepers actively take part in their team’s build up. They have good passing ability using which they help their team in progressing the ball forward when in possession. Some keepers have the capability of playing between high defensive lines, settling in between the centre-backs to receive the ball and play it in space where they have a spare man waiting. They are also useful in changing flanks. The shot-stopping ability of most proactive keepers is above average. They are also good enough in intercepting and breaking up attacks as well as having control over their box.
Example: Robert Sánchez, Aaron Ramsdale



Complete Goalkeepers

Complete Goalkeepers possess all the facets of the modern game integrated into their palette. They are very good at stopping shots, can read the play and cover their defensive line in an elite way, have the ability to play like an outfield player and alternate short play and long play under pressure with impressive accuracy.
Example: Alisson



Libero Goalkeepers

The word ‘libero’ stands for ‘free’ in Italian. A Libero Goalkeeper is ever-present as an extra man behind the defenders to serve as a passing option. Libero is the player that most of the deep build-up starts from. They are able to circulate the ball even under pressure and can transform any situation into an attack. Also performing great at keeping a high line by controlling the zone behind the defensive line. Most of them though, tend not to be dominant in the penalty area, helping the team way higher up the pitch, in and out of possession.
Example: Ederson



Reactive Goalkeepers

Reactive Goalkeepers possess average qualities in most areas. Instead of anticipating the danger beforehand, these GKs rely on their instincts when they sense danger. They are not comfortable with the ball at their feet. Instead, they tend to go long to reach the more advanced zones with their passing. Even though some of them are above average shot-stoppers, they have almost no sweeping skills and tend not to leave the security of their penalty area to take part in defensive actions.
Example: Hugo Lloris, Łukasz Fabiański



Ball Playing Goalkeepers and Sweeper Keepers

There are no Goalkeepers in the Premier League that excel only in ball-playing. GKs like Alisson and Ederson are excellent players on the ball but both of them are part of different keeper groups. They have their own skillset to go alongside the ball playing. Although we can consider Vicente Guaita for this one as he has no other remarkable traits in other areas.

Similarly, there are no out and out sweeper keepers as well. Ramsdale and Ederson are adept at sweeping up behind their defences. But again, here we see both of them belong to different clusters. Here we may consider Emiliano Martinez or Jordan Pickford, but they are far off the mark of sweeper set by no other than Manuel Neuer.
